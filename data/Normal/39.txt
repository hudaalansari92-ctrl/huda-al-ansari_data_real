Karen, a 57-year-old woman with a long-standing history of hyperthyroidism due to Graves' disease, presented with palpitations, chest pain, and episodes of lightheadedness. Given that hyperthyroidism can increase the risk of arrhythmias and heart disease, she was concerned about coronary artery disease (CAD). Blood tests, including troponin and thyroid hormone levels, were conducted. While her thyroid hormones were elevated, indicating uncontrolled hyperthyroidism, her troponin levels were normal. Her ECG showed signs of atrial fibrillation, a common complication of hyperthyroidism, but no evidence of ischemia. An echocardiogram showed a normal ejection fraction with no signs of reduced blood flow to the heart. After a stress test ruled out CAD, Karen was diagnosed with hyperthyroidism-induced palpitations, not coronary artery disease. Her treatment was adjusted with anti-thyroid medications and beta-blockers to control her heart rate, which improved her symptoms.
