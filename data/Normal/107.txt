Khaled, a 50-year-old man, began experiencing crushing chest pain that radiated to his left arm and worsened with exertion, leading him to believe he had coronary artery disease. He went to the hospital, where he had an electrocardiogram (ECG) and a stress test, both of which were normal. Cardiac enzyme tests showed no signs of a heart attack, and a coronary angiogram revealed no blockages in the arteries. Despite the persistence of symptoms, coronary artery disease was ruled out, and after further testing, he was diagnosed with coronary artery spasm, a condition that causes temporary narrowing of the coronary arteries, leading to symptoms similar to angina, despite no permanent blockage. Khaled was started on medications such as calcium channel blockers and long-acting nitrates to help prevent the spasms, along with lifestyle changes such as avoiding stress, cutting out caffeine, and doing light exercise. After a few weeks, he noticed a significant improvement in his symptoms, confirming that the diagnosis was not related to blocked arteries, but rather to spasms of the arteries that led to symptoms similar to angina.
