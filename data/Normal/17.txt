Frank, a 68-year-old male with a 20-year history of rheumatoid arthritis (RA), experienced occasional chest pain and palpitations. Given that RA is associated with chronic inflammation, which can increase the risk of coronary artery disease, his doctor performed a thorough evaluation. Blood tests, including inflammatory markers and cardiac enzymes, were normal. His ECG showed mild, non-specific changes, but a stress echocardiogram revealed no signs of reduced blood flow to the heart. Coronary angiography confirmed that there were no significant blockages in his arteries. Frank was diagnosed with costochondritis, an inflammation of the chest wall common in patients with RA, rather than coronary artery disease. He was treated with anti-inflammatory medications, and his symptoms resolved. Despite the chronic inflammation from RA, his heart remained healthy.
