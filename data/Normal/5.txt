John, a 50-year-old construction worker, sought medical care after experiencing sharp, localized chest pain that worsened with physical activity and certain movements. The pain had started after a day of heavy lifting on the job. Concerned about a potential heart problem, he underwent blood tests and an ECG, both of which were normal. A physical examination revealed that the pain was reproducible when pressure was applied to his chest wall and that his symptoms were exacerbated by movement rather than being constant. John was diagnosed with a muscular strain, likely due to overexertion at work. His chest pain was musculoskeletal, not cardiac in nature. He was advised to rest and use anti-inflammatory medications, which resolved his symptoms over the next few weeks.
