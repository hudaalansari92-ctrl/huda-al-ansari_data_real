Emily, a 64-year-old woman, had been living with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) for over 15 years. Her SLE was managed with immunosuppressive therapy, but she experienced frequent flare-ups characterized by joint pain and fatigue. Recently, she developed mild chest pain and shortness of breath, raising concerns about heart disease. SLE is associated with chronic inflammation, which can accelerate atherosclerosis and increase the risk of coronary artery disease (CAD). Emily underwent a full cardiac workup, including blood tests, an ECG, and a stress echocardiogram. Her troponin levels were normal, and her stress test showed no signs of ischemia. Coronary angiography revealed no significant blockages, and her echocardiogram showed normal heart function. Despite her high risk of CAD due to SLE, Emily was found to have no coronary artery disease. Her chest pain was attributed to pleuritis, an inflammation of the lining of the lungs, which is common in lupus patients. She was treated with anti-inflammatory medications, and her symptoms resolved.
