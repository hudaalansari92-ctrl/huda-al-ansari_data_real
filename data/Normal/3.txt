Tom, a 60-year-old male, arrived at the emergency department with sharp chest pain that worsened with movement and deep breaths. He was concerned it might be a heart attack, as the pain radiated to his shoulder. After conducting blood tests and ECGs, his troponin levels were normal, and no signs of coronary artery disease were detected. Further physical examination revealed tenderness in his chest wall along the costosternal joints. He was diagnosed with costochondritis, an inflammation of the cartilage where the ribs meet the sternum. Tom's chest pain was musculoskeletal in origin and not related to heart disease. He was prescribed anti-inflammatory medications and advised to rest, which significantly improved his condition over time.
