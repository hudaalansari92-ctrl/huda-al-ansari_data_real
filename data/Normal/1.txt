David, a 52-year-old man, visited the emergency department after experiencing chest pain and a burning sensation behind his breastbone, particularly after meals. 
The pain sometimes radiated to his back and was worse when lying down. He feared it might be heart-related, 
as the discomfort resembled the chest pain often associated with coronary artery disease (CAD). 
Blood tests showed no elevated troponin levels, and his ECG revealed no signs of ischemia or other heart abnormalities. 
An echocardiogram confirmed that his heart function was normal. Upon further evaluation, David was diagnosed with gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD). 
His symptoms were due to stomach acid irritating the lining of the esophagus, not heart disease. 
He was prescribed proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) and given dietary recommendations, which helped alleviate his symptoms.
