A 62-year-old male patient with a significant family history of coronary artery disease (CAD) presented for evaluation despite having no clinical signs of CAD. His father had suffered a myocardial infarction at age 54, and his brother was diagnosed with CAD at 58. The patient himself was a long-term smoker, had hypertension managed with medication, and exhibited elevated cholesterol levels. He also had a sedentary lifestyle and was classified as obese based on his body mass index (BMI). Despite these risk factors, he underwent a thorough cardiac evaluation, including stress testing and coronary angiography, which revealed normal coronary arteries with no evidence of significant stenosis. This case highlights the complexity of cardiovascular risk assessment; even patients with multiple risk factors can be free of CAD, underscoring the need for individualized patient management and the limitations of risk factor profiling in predicting coronary outcomes.
