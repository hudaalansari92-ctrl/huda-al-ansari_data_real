A 50-year-old woman living in an industrial area with high levels of air pollution and particulate matter visited her doctor, complaining of frequent shortness of breath, fatigue, and occasional chest tightness. She had no traditional coronary artery disease (CAD) risk factors; her blood pressure, cholesterol levels, and body weight were all within normal ranges, and she had no family history of heart disease. However, her proximity to a major freeway and multiple factories exposed her to significant levels of air pollutants daily. Concerned about the cardiovascular impact of this long-term exposure, her physician ordered several tests, including a coronary calcium scan, which showed no evidence of CAD at this time. Still, the doctor explained that chronic exposure to air pollution can cause ongoing inflammation in blood vessels, potentially leading to a higher future risk of heart issues. She was advised to take steps to reduce exposure, such as using an air purifier at home and avoiding outdoor exercise on high-pollution days, while continuing with regular monitoring to detect any early signs of cardiovascular changes.
