Tom, a 58-year-old man with a 30-year history of smoking, had been diagnosed with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) five years ago. He often experienced shortness of breath and chest discomfort, particularly after physical exertion, which raised concerns about his heart health. Given that COPD and smoking are both risk factors for coronary artery disease, Tom underwent a series of tests, including blood tests, an ECG, and an echocardiogram. His troponin levels were normal, and his ECG showed no signs of ischemia. A coronary CT angiogram was performed and revealed no significant blockages in his coronary arteries. Despite his COPD and smoking history, Tom did not have coronary artery disease. His chest discomfort was related to his lung disease, and his symptoms improved with the use of bronchodilators and inhaled corticosteroids to manage his COPD.
