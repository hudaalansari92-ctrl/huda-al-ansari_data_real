Ziad, who is 48 years old, suffers from sharp chest pain that appears suddenly and is described as stabbing or burning, concentrated on the right side of the chest and extending to the right shoulder. He also complains of shortness of breath that worsens when lying down and mild leg swelling. The symptoms appear intermittently and are not related to physical exertion. There is no previous history of heart disease. Still, the patient suffers from high blood pressure that is controlled with medication, has a history of obesity (body mass index of 30), and a sedentary lifestyle. The patient is a non-smoker. The initial tests showed normal ECG and stress test results, and the heart enzymes and cholesterol were within normal ranges. The chest X-ray and coronary angiography did not show any blockage in the coronary arteries. After excluding coronary artery disease, the patient was diagnosed with pulmonary embolism, where a blood clot in the lung can cause similar symptoms, such as chest pain, shortness of breath, and leg swelling due to blockage of the pulmonary blood vessels. Anticoagulants such as heparin or warfarin were prescribed to prevent the formation of additional clots, along with advice to reduce the risk of clot formation, such as increasing physical activity and avoiding prolonged sitting. Coordination was also made with a cardiologist to monitor the condition and ensure no other complications. In severe cases, treatments such as thrombolysis may be used. (Thrombolysis).
