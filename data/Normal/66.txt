A 38-year-old man visited his physician complaining of frequent fatigue, occasional chest tightness, and difficulty concentrating. Although he had no traditional risk factors for coronary artery disease (CAD), he was a non-smoker, maintained a normal weight, and had normal blood pressure and cholesterol levels; he admitted to a long-standing habit of consuming highly processed, high-fat, and salty foods. His daily diet consisted mainly of fast food, sugary drinks, and snacks high in saturated fats and salt, with minimal intake of fruits and vegetables. Concerned about his symptoms and dietary habits, his doctor conducted a series of cardiovascular tests, including a stress test, which showed no evidence of CAD but indicated early signs of endothelial dysfunction, a precursor to potential heart problems. The physician explained that a diet high in saturated fats, salt, and sugar can lead to inflammation and damage to blood vessels, increasing his risk of future cardiovascular disease. He was advised to transition to a balanced, heart-healthy diet and schedule regular follow-ups to monitor his heart health.
