Linda, a 61-year-old woman living with Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) for over 20 years, had a well-controlled viral load but remained at increased risk for coronary artery disease (CAD) due to chronic inflammation associated with the condition. She recently began experiencing chest discomfort and fatigue, especially during moderate physical activity. Given the known link between HIV and the early onset of cardiovascular disease, her physician ordered blood tests, an ECG, and a stress echocardiogram. Her troponin levels were normal, and her ECG showed no signs of ischemia. The stress test revealed normal heart function, and coronary angiography showed no significant blockages in her coronary arteries. Linda was diagnosed with non-cardiac chest pain, likely related to muscle strain rather than coronary artery disease. She was counseled on maintaining her heart health through regular exercise, a healthy diet, and continued control of her HIV with antiretroviral therapy.
