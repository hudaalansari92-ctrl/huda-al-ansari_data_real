A 51-year-old school teacher living in an urban area with frequent air quality warnings reported a recurring sensation of tightness in her chest, along with headaches and general fatigue, particularly on days following high pollution. With no signs of coronary artery disease and healthy blood pressure and cholesterol levels, her doctor attributed her symptoms to long-term exposure to PM2.5, leading to oxidative stress and systemic inflammation. These factors are known to contribute to endothelial dysfunction, an early event in atherosclerosis development. She was advised to use air filters at home, avoid outdoor activities during peak pollution hours, and increase her intake of vitamins C and E for their antioxidant properties.
