John, a 65-year-old man with Parkinson's disease, started experiencing chest pain and shortness of breath during routine activities like walking. Concerned about the possibility of coronary artery disease (CAD), especially due to his age and reduced physical activity related to Parkinson's, his doctor ordered a series of tests. Blood work, including troponin levels, was normal, and an ECG showed no signs of ischemia. An echocardiogram revealed normal heart function, with an ejection fraction of 58%. A stress test confirmed that John did not have CAD. His chest pain was attributed to muscle stiffness and postural changes due to his Parkinson's disease. John was advised to continue his Parkinson's therapy and was referred to physical therapy to improve his posture and reduce musculoskeletal discomfort.
