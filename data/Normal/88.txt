A 50-year-old man, a resident in a high-traffic urban neighborhood, began experiencing recurring fatigue, occasional headaches, and mild chest discomfort. With no personal or family history of coronary artery disease (CAD) and no other cardiovascular risk factors, he initially attributed his symptoms to stress. However, his doctor suspected that prolonged exposure to particulate matter (PM2.5) from traffic pollution was causing oxidative stress and systemic inflammation, which could lead to endothelial dysfunction and eventually increase his CAD risk. Although he showed no signs of CAD on initial tests, he was encouraged to use indoor air filters, reduce outdoor activities during high pollution times, and consume anti-inflammatory foods to reduce his risk of developing atherosclerosis over time.
