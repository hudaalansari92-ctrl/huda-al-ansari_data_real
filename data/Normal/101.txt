Hassan, a 50-year-old factory worker, has spent over 25 years in a heavy industrial environment, regularly exposed to air pollution, chemical fumes, and high noise levels. His physically demanding job, combined with long hours and stressful conditions, has contributed to significant health risks. Living in a densely populated urban area with high pollution levels and limited access to healthy food further exacerbated his condition. Despite no family history of heart disease, he began experiencing frequent headaches, shortness of breath, fatigue, and occasional chest discomfort, particularly after long shifts. Concerned about his symptoms, he sought medical attention. Diagnostic tests revealed elevated LDL cholesterol and high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hsCRP), indicating chronic inflammation, while an ECG showed minor irregularities. A stress test suggested reduced exercise tolerance, and his physician identified workplace and environmental factors as contributing risks to his cardiovascular health. Though he had not yet developed full-blown coronary artery disease (CAD), he was diagnosed with a high cardiovascular risk due to prolonged exposure to pollution, occupational stress, poor diet, and lack of physical activity. His treatment plan included workplace adjustments, such as wearing protective masks and taking frequent breaks, along with lifestyle modifications, including regular light exercise, stress management, and dietary improvements. Medications, including statins, were prescribed to manage cholesterol, and he was advised to monitor his blood pressure. After six months, his condition improved significantly, his cholesterol levels decreased, he experienced fewer symptoms, and he incorporated light physical activity into his routine. He also explored alternative job roles with less physical strain and pollution exposure.
