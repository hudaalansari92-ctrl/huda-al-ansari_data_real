James, a 58-year-old construction worker, had a history of high cholesterol and hypertension but had been inconsistent with medications. He worked long hours in a physically demanding job, often in stressful conditions, and had a poor diet, relying on fast food and processed meals. He began experiencing episodes of chest pain and shortness of breath while at work, especially after lifting heavy objects. Initially, he ignored these symptoms, attributing them to the physical nature of his job. Blood tests at the hospital revealed elevated troponin levels (TnI: 1.2 ng/mL), indicating heart muscle injury. His lipid profile showed an LDL cholesterol of 160 mg/dL and a total cholesterol of 260 mg/dL. His ECG revealed ST-segment depression, indicating ischemia, and an echocardiogram showed a mildly reduced ejection fraction of 50% with hypokinesis in the anterior wall. A coronary angiogram revealed a 75% blockage in his left anterior descending (LAD) artery, and James underwent a percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement. Post-procedure, his chest pain resolved, and he was prescribed statins, beta-blockers, and aspirin. Lifestyle changes, including dietary modifications and stress management, were strongly recommended, especially given the physical and stressful nature of his work environment.
