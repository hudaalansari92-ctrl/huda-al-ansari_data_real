A 45-year-old woman with a history of ankylosing spondylitis (AS), a chronic autoimmune disease affecting the spine and causing inflammation in the joints, visited her physician with complaints of chest pain and shortness of breath. She had no traditional coronary artery disease (CAD) risk factors, she was a non-smoker, had a normal BMI, and her cholesterol and blood pressure were within healthy ranges. However, her AS had been active for many years, leading to chronic systemic inflammation that affected her overall health. Concerned, her doctor ordered coronary angiography, which revealed significant CAD with a 65% blockage in one of her coronary arteries, requiring stent placement. Her cardiologist explained that the persistent inflammation from ankylosing spondylitis likely contributed to the development of CAD, as inflammation can accelerate the buildup of plaque in the arteries. She was advised to continue managing her AS aggressively, adopt a heart-healthy lifestyle, and schedule regular follow-ups to monitor her heart health closely.
