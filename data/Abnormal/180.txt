Sami, a 60-year-old retired government employee, was diagnosed with coronary artery disease and suffered a minor heart attack due to narrowing of the coronary arteries. Sami has a family history of heart disease, as his father died of a heart attack at the age of 58, and his mother suffers from diabetes. He had been a smoker for 35 years, consuming a pack of cigarettes daily, and suffered from obesity (body mass index of 31) with a sedentary lifestyle. His chronic diseases included hypertension, which was diagnosed 15 years ago, and type 2 diabetes for 8 years. He began to experience chest pain and shortness of breath when exerting himself. His condition worsened when he felt sharp chest pain that lasted for more than 20 minutes, prompting his family to take him to the emergency room. The tests revealed myocardial ischemia and a minor heart attack, and the cardiac catheterization showed an 80% narrowing in the left anterior descending coronary artery and a 60% narrowing in the right coronary artery. Sami received emergency treatment with thrombolytic drugs, and a therapeutic catheterization was performed with the placement of a stent in the left coronary artery. The treatment included changing his lifestyle by quitting smoking, following a healthy diet, exercising, and losing weight. Additionally, cholesterol-lowering medications, beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, and antiplatelet drugs were prescribed, along with adjustments to diabetes medication dosages. He enrolled in a cardiac rehabilitation program to improve his health condition. After 6 months, his condition improved significantly; cholesterol and blood pressure levels decreased, he quit smoking, and lost some excess weight.
