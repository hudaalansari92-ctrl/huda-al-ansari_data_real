Robert, a 63-year-old male with a 20-year history of poorly controlled type 2 diabetes, presented to the clinic with worsening chest pain and fatigue. His sedentary lifestyle, along with fluctuating blood sugar levels, had made it difficult for him to stay active, and he had also gained weight over the past decade. Robert's diabetes had been poorly managed, with an HbA1c of 8.9%, indicating consistently elevated blood sugar. Over time, this had led to damage to his blood vessels, increasing his risk of developing coronary artery disease (CAD). Diabetes is one of the leading risk factors for coronary heart disease because it accelerates atherosclerosis, a condition where plaque builds up in the arteries. Robert experienced chest discomfort during physical activities like walking or mowing the lawn, which he initially ignored. However, the symptoms worsened, and he began experiencing chest pain even at rest. An ECG showed non-specific ST-segment changes, and blood tests revealed elevated LDL cholesterol (170 mg/dL) and fasting glucose of 190 mg/dL. His echocardiogram revealed an ejection fraction of 50%, and a coronary angiogram identified a 75% blockage in the left anterior descending artery. Robert underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement and was started on insulin to better control his diabetes, along with statins, aspirin, and Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors. Post-treatment, he was referred to a diabetes educator and a nutritionist to improve his diet and manage his blood sugar levels more effectively, which significantly reduced his risk of future heart attacks.
