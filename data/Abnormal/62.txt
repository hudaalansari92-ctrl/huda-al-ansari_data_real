Helen, a 60-year-old woman with a 15-year history of rheumatoid arthritis (RA), had been on long-term immunosuppressive therapy to control her symptoms. RA is associated with chronic systemic inflammation, which increases the risk of developing coronary artery disease. Helen began experiencing chest pain during routine activities such as walking and household chores, which she initially thought was fatigue related to her arthritis. Blood tests revealed elevated troponin levels (TnI: 0.6 ng/mL), and her ECG showed non-specific ST-T wave changes. An echocardiogram showed an ejection fraction of 55%, but with mild hypokinesis of the inferior wall, suggesting reduced blood flow. Coronary angiography revealed a 75% blockage in her right coronary artery. Helen underwent PCI with stent placement and was started on statins, aspirin, and beta-blockers to manage her heart disease. Her rheumatologist adjusted her RA medication to better control the inflammation, reducing her long-term cardiovascular risk.
