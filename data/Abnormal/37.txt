Mary, a 68-year-old woman with a 20-year history of type 2 diabetes and high cholesterol, presented with worsening chest discomfort over several months. Her chest pain occurred during physical exertion, such as walking, and resolved with rest. Her ECG at rest showed no major abnormalities, but her stress test demonstrated ST-segment depression, indicating ischemia during exercise. Blood tests showed elevated LDL cholesterol and mildly elevated fasting blood glucose. An echocardiogram revealed normal left ventricular function but detected mild diastolic dysfunction. Coronary angiography revealed multiple areas of narrowing, including an 80% blockage in the right coronary artery and a 70% blockage in the left circumflex artery. Given the extent of her coronary artery disease, Mary underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement in both arteries. After the procedure, her chest pain resolved, and follow-up blood tests showed improved cholesterol levels after starting statins. She was placed on medications to manage her diabetes, cholesterol, and blood pressure, including metformin, statins, and Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors. A follow-up echocardiogram three months later showed stable heart function.
