Tom, a 58-year-old male with a 30-year history of smoking, had been diagnosed with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) five years ago. Despite quitting smoking recently, he continued to suffer from frequent shortness of breath and fatigue. He was also on medication for hypertension. Tom's COPD, coupled with a long history of smoking, had greatly increased his risk for coronary heart disease. COPD is associated with systemic inflammation, which can contribute to the development of atherosclerosis and increase the risk of coronary artery disease. Tom initially thought his worsening shortness of breath was related to his COPD, but when he started experiencing chest pain during mild exertion, such as walking to the mailbox, he sought medical help. Blood tests revealed elevated troponin levels (1.2 ng/mL), and his lipid profile showed an LDL of 180 mg/dL. An ECG revealed ischemic changes; an echocardiogram showed reduced heart function with an ejection fraction of 40%. Coronary angiography revealed significant multi-vessel disease, including an 85% blockage in his right coronary artery. Tom underwent coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) surgery due to the severity of his blockages. Post-surgery, his symptoms improved, and he was placed on long-term statins, beta-blockers, and aspirin. He was also enrolled in a pulmonary rehabilitation program to manage his COPD, which helped improve his overall quality of life.
