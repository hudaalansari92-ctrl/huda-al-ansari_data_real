A 60-year-old man with a history of severe obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) presented to the emergency department with chest discomfort and shortness of breath. Although he had no traditional coronary artery disease (CAD) risk factors, he was a non-smoker, had normal cholesterol levels, and managed his weight well; he had struggled with untreated sleep apnea for years. Nightly episodes of oxygen desaturation and loud snoring, reported by his wife, had led to chronic fatigue and morning headaches. A coronary angiogram conducted at the hospital revealed significant CAD with 75% blockage in one of his major coronary arteries, requiring a stent. His cardiologist explained that the untreated OSA likely contributed to elevated blood pressure surges and inflammation, accelerating the atherosclerotic process. Post-procedure, he was prescribed continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) therapy to manage his sleep apnea. He was advised to make lifestyle adjustments to reduce his cardiovascular risk, including regular monitoring and follow-up care.
