Layla, a 45-year-old teacher, has been living with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) for over 15 years and systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) for five years, both of which contribute to chronic systemic inflammation and increase her risk of vascular damage. Due to long-term corticosteroid therapy, she has experienced weight gain and mild hypertension. Her sedentary lifestyle, caused by joint pain, and her reliance on processed foods due to fatigue further compounded her health risks. Over the past several months, she began experiencing frequent chest discomfort, shortness of breath, unusual fatigue, palpitations, and dizziness, initially attributing them to stress and medication side effects. However, as symptoms worsened, she sought medical evaluation. Diagnostic tests revealed elevated LDL cholesterol, high inflammatory markers (CRP and ESR), minor ischemic changes on ECG, reduced exercise tolerance on a stress test, and a 60% narrowing in the left anterior descending (LAD) artery, indicating early-stage coronary artery disease (CAD). Her treatment plan focused on reducing systemic inflammation through medication adjustments, adopting an anti-inflammatory diet, engaging in low-impact exercises like swimming and yoga, and managing cardiovascular risk factors with statins, low-dose aspirin, and beta-blockers. After six months, Layla's symptoms improved, her cholesterol levels stabilized, and she was able to integrate gentle physical activity into her routine.
