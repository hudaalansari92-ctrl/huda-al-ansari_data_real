Paul, a 59-year-old male with a history of high cholesterol and hypertension, had been experiencing stable angina for the past six months, characterized by chest discomfort during exertion that resolved with rest. His ECG showed no significant changes at rest, but during a stress test, ST-segment depression was noted, indicating ischemia. Blood tests revealed elevated LDL cholesterol but normal troponin levels, suggesting no active heart muscle damage. An echocardiogram revealed preserved left ventricular function with no regional wall motion abnormalities. Coronary angiography later showed a 75% blockage in his left anterior descending artery (LAD). Given his stable condition, Paul was treated with medical therapy, including statins, nitrates, aspirin, and beta-blockers, instead of immediate stenting. After lifestyle changes, including a heart-healthy diet and exercise, along with medication, Paul's symptoms improved, and a follow-up stress test showed reduced ischemia. He remained under close monitoring for future risk.
