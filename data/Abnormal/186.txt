Kareem, a 54-year-old factory worker, has spent over 30 years in a steel manufacturing plant, where he is constantly exposed to air pollution, dust, smoke, and industrial chemicals. His physically demanding job requires long hours of standing, heavy lifting, and working in extreme temperatures. Additionally, he lives near the factory in an area with poor air quality, further increasing his exposure to environmental pollutants. Despite having no family history of heart disease, his unhealthy lifestyle, characterized by a lack of exercise, a high-fat and high-sodium diet, and smoking a pack of cigarettes daily for over 20 years, significantly raised his cardiovascular risk. Over the past year, Kareem experienced frequent chest discomfort, which he initially dismissed as work-related fatigue. However, the discomfort worsened, developing into a tight, squeezing pain in his chest, especially after strenuous tasks. He also suffered from shortness of breath, dizziness, and heart palpitations, eventually prompting him to seek medical attention. Diagnostic tests revealed high LDL cholesterol, low HDL cholesterol, increased inflammatory markers, mild ischemic changes on ECG, and significant arterial blockages: 65% in the left anterior descending (LAD) artery and 50% in the right coronary artery (RCA), leading to a diagnosis of early-stage coronary artery disease (CAD). His work environment and unhealthy habits played a major role in accelerating vascular damage and increasing inflammation. His treatment plan included workplace adjustments such as wearing protective masks and taking more frequent breaks, adopting a heart-healthy diet, engaging in light daily exercise, and participating in a smoking cessation program. He was also prescribed statins, beta-blockers, and aspirin to manage his condition. After six months, Kareem made significant progress, he reduced smoking, improved his diet, lost 4 kg, and experienced better endurance with fewer chest discomfort episodes.
