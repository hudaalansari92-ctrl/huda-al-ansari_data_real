Susan, a 55-year-old female with a long-standing history of type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and hyperlipidemia, began experiencing mild chest discomfort that progressively worsened over a few months. Initially triggered by exertion, such as climbing stairs, the discomfort eventually occurred even at rest. Blood tests revealed elevated troponin levels (TnI: 0.8 ng/mL), indicating mild myocardial injury, while her lipid profile showed elevated LDL cholesterol (180 mg/dL), total cholesterol (270 mg/dL), and triglycerides (240 mg/dL). Her HbA1c was 8.2%, indicating poorly controlled diabetes. A resting ECG showed non-specific ST-segment changes, but a stress test revealed ST-segment depression in the inferior leads, indicating ischemia during exertion. An echocardiogram showed mild left ventricular hypertrophy due to her long-standing hypertension and a mildly reduced ejection fraction of 50%, with hypokinesis in the inferior wall, indicating reduced blood flow. Susan was diagnosed with stable angina caused by multi-vessel coronary artery disease. A coronary angiogram revealed significant blockages, including an 85% blockage in the right coronary artery and a 70% blockage in the left circumflex artery, requiring percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement in both arteries. Post-procedure, Susan was started on aspirin, clopidogrel, high-dose statins, and metformin to control her diabetes. Following the intervention, her chest pain resolved, and a stress test six months later showed no signs of ischemia. Her HbA1c and cholesterol levels improved with medication and lifestyle changes, and a follow-up echocardiogram demonstrated stable heart function with no further deterioration in her ejection fraction.
