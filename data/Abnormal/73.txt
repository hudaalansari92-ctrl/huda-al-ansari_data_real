Linda, a 61-year-old woman who had been living with Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) for over 20 years, developed chest pain and shortness of breath during mild exertion. Although her HIV was well-controlled with antiretroviral therapy, long-term inflammation associated with the condition increased her risk of developing coronary artery disease (CAD). Blood tests revealed elevated troponin levels, and her ECG showed ischemic changes. An echocardiogram revealed an ejection fraction of 50%, and a coronary angiogram revealed an 80% blockage in the left circumflex artery. Linda was diagnosed with CAD and underwent PCI with stent placement. After the procedure, her symptoms improved, and she was prescribed statins, beta-blockers, and aspirin. She continued her antiretroviral therapy and was closely monitored for both her HIV and heart health.
