Eleanor, a 70-year-old retired nurse, had a history of high blood pressure, high cholesterol, and mild angina, which she managed with medication. For years, she experienced occasional chest pain during physical activity, such as walking uphill or gardening, but it would subside with rest. One day, she noticed the chest discomfort was becoming more frequent and lasting longer, even with less exertion. Concerned, she visited her cardiologist, who performed a stress test, revealing ischemic changes and reduced blood flow to the heart muscle. A coronary angiogram showed significant blockages in two coronary arteries: an 80% blockage in the left circumflex artery and a 75% blockage in the right coronary artery. Given her age and the stable nature of her angina, Eleanor's cardiologist recommended medical management over surgery. She was prescribed beta-blockers to reduce the workload on her heart, statins to lower cholesterol, and nitrates to relieve chest pain. Eleanor also participated in a cardiac rehabilitation program, which focused on supervised exercise and dietary changes. Over time, her angina symptoms improved, and her risk of a heart attack was reduced through consistent medication use and lifestyle modifications.
