A 53-year-old man with a 15-year history of systemic sclerosis (scleroderma), an autoimmune disease causing hardening and inflammation of the skin and connective tissues, presented with chest discomfort and unusual fatigue. He had no traditional coronary artery disease (CAD) risk factors, he did not smoke, had a healthy body weight, and his blood pressure and cholesterol levels were within normal ranges. However, systemic sclerosis had led to chronic inflammation and occasional flare-ups, affecting his vascular health. Concerned about his symptoms, his doctor conducted an ECG and coronary angiography, which showed significant CAD with a 70% blockage in a major coronary artery, requiring stent placement. His cardiologist explained that the persistent vascular inflammation from systemic sclerosis likely accelerated the development of atherosclerosis, despite the absence of other risk factors. Post-procedure, he was advised to closely manage his autoimmune condition, incorporate anti-inflammatory foods into his diet, and undergo regular cardiovascular check-ups to monitor his heart health and reduce the risk of future complications.
