Sarah, a 45-year-old woman living in a rural farming community, has been smoking regularly for over 20 years. In her town, smoking is a common habit, and access to smoking cessation programs or resources is very limited. Due to the cultural normalization of smoking and the lack of healthcare outreach, Sarah never felt the need to quit. Over time, her heavy smoking leads to significant damage to the walls of her arteries, causing the buildup of fatty plaques. This condition, known as atherosclerosis, slowly narrows her arteries, restricting blood flow to her heart. Additionally, Sarah regularly consumes alcohol, another risk factor that further strains her cardiovascular system. She begins experiencing occasional chest tightness and shortness of breath, especially after light physical activity, but she brushes off these symptoms. One day, after feeling unusually fatigued and dizzy, she visits the nearest clinic, which is 30 miles away. Tests reveal that Sarah's arteries are severely narrowed, and she is diagnosed with coronary artery disease (CAD). Her smoking and alcohol use over the years were major contributors to her condition, and she now faces an increased risk of heart attack without immediate lifestyle changes and medical intervention.
