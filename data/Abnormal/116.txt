A 55-year-old woman with a 15-year history of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), an autoimmune disease characterized by chronic inflammation, arrived at her cardiologist's office with complaints of persistent chest pain, fatigue, and shortness of breath on exertion. Although she had no traditional risk factors for coronary artery disease (CAD), she was a non-smoker, had normal cholesterol levels, and maintained a healthy blood pressure; her lupus had been active for years, resulting in systemic inflammation affecting multiple organs. Concerned by her symptoms, her cardiologist ordered an ECG and coronary angiography, which showed a 65% blockage in one of her major coronary arteries, confirming CAD and necessitating stent placement. Her doctor explained that the chronic inflammation associated with lupus likely accelerated atherosclerosis, even in the absence of traditional risk factors. After the procedure, she was advised to manage her lupus proactively, adopt a heart-healthy lifestyle, and attend regular follow-ups to monitor her cardiovascular health and prevent future complications.
