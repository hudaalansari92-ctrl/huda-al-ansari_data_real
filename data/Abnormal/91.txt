A 46-year-old man, a business owner, was admitted to the hospital after experiencing chest pain and shortness of breath during a stressful meeting. Despite having no traditional risk factors for coronary artery disease (CAD), he was a non-smoker, had a normal BMI, and had no family history of heart disease, and he had been under intense chronic psychological stress for years due to the pressures of managing his company. His coping mechanisms included poor dietary habits, excessive caffeine intake, and limited physical activity. An electrocardiogram (ECG) and coronary angiography revealed significant coronary artery disease, with a 70% blockage in the left anterior descending artery, requiring stent placement. The cardiologist attributed his premature CAD to the cumulative effects of chronic stress, which likely contributed to vascular inflammation and accelerated atherosclerosis. Post-treatment, he was advised to adopt stress-reduction techniques, improve his diet, and engage in regular exercise to reduce the risk of further cardiac events.
