A 58-year-old woman with a long-standing diagnosis of Sjogren's syndrome, an autoimmune disorder causing dryness and chronic inflammation in various organs, visited her cardiologist after experiencing chest pain and palpitations during mild exertion. She had no traditional coronary artery disease (CAD) risk factors, she was a non-smoker, had a healthy weight, and maintained normal cholesterol and blood pressure levels. However, her Sjogren's syndrome had led to years of systemic inflammation, affecting not only her salivary and tear glands but also contributing to vascular inflammation. Coronary angiography revealed a 60% blockage in one of her coronary arteries, confirming CAD that required stent placement. Her cardiologist explained that chronic inflammation from Sjogren's syndrome likely played a key role in accelerating atherosclerosis. She was advised to work closely with her rheumatologist to manage inflammation, incorporate heart-healthy practices, and schedule regular cardiovascular follow-ups to reduce the risk of future heart complications.
