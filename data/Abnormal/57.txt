Helen, a 66-year-old woman with a 15-year history of rheumatoid arthritis (RA), experienced frequent joint pain and swelling, which was managed with immunosuppressive therapy. She recently started noticing chest discomfort, especially during physical activity, such as walking and gardening. Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic inflammatory disease that significantly increases the risk of coronary heart disease due to chronic systemic inflammation, which can accelerate the formation of atherosclerotic plaques in the arteries. Helen initially ignored her symptoms, attributing them to fatigue from RA. However, after experiencing chest pain that radiated to her left arm, she went to the emergency room. Blood tests revealed elevated troponin levels (0.6 ng/mL), and her lipid profile showed an LDL cholesterol of 165 mg/dL. An ECG showed signs of ischemia, and an echocardiogram revealed a reduced ejection fraction of 50%. Coronary angiography identified a 70% blockage in her left anterior descending artery. Helen underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement. Post-procedure, she was started on aspirin, statins, and medications to better control her inflammation. A multidisciplinary approach involving her rheumatologist helped optimize her RA treatment to minimize the risk of further cardiovascular complications.
