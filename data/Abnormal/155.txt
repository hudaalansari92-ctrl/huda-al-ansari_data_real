A 57-year-old male named Mr. Johnson lived in a city with high levels of air pollution due to industrial emissions. He presented to the emergency department with severe chest pain and shortness of breath after experiencing a sudden episode while gardening.
Mr. Johnson described his chest pain as a sharp, stabbing sensation that radiated to his left arm. He also reported feeling lightheaded and having difficulty catching his breath.
He had a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, which were poorly managed. He also had a previous diagnosis of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) two years prior, which raised concerns about his coagulation status.
His risk factors included long-term exposure to air pollutants, particularly particulate matter (PM2.5) and nitrogen dioxide (NO2), from living near factories and heavy traffic. He also had a family history of heart disease; his father had suffered a heart attack at age 60.
Diagnostic tests revealed significant coronary artery disease with evidence of thrombosis in one of the coronary arteries. Mr. Johnson was treated with anticoagulants and underwent angioplasty to remove the clot. He was advised on lifestyle changes, including dietary modifications and reducing exposure to pollution.
