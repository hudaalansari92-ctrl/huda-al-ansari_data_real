Eleanor, a 72-year-old woman with a history of hypertension and mild hyperlipidemia, came in for a routine check-up. She reported mild fatigue but denied any chest pain, shortness of breath, or other typical symptoms of heart disease. Routine blood tests revealed mildly elevated troponin levels (TnI: 0.3 ng/mL), indicating low-grade heart muscle injury, and her lipid panel showed an LDL cholesterol level of 140 mg/dL and total cholesterol of 220 mg/dL. Her ECG displayed non-specific ST-T wave changes but no significant abnormalities. An echocardiogram revealed an ejection fraction of 55%, with mild hypokinesis of the lateral wall, indicating reduced movement in that region. Despite the absence of major symptoms, Eleanor was diagnosed with silent coronary artery disease, where significant blockages occur without noticeable symptoms. A coronary angiogram confirmed an 80% blockage in the left circumflex artery, and she underwent a successful percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement. She was prescribed statins, aspirin, and blood pressure medications, along with recommended lifestyle changes. Despite the lack of classic symptoms, Eleanor's condition was detected early, and a follow-up echocardiogram six months later showed stable heart function. Her LDL cholesterol levels also dropped to 90 mg/dL with statin therapy.
