Mary, a 65-year-old woman with a 15-year history of type 2 diabetes, high cholesterol, and obesity, lived a largely sedentary lifestyle. Her father had died of a heart attack at 60, adding to her risk factors. She began experiencing fatigue and shortness of breath during daily activities like walking or gardening, which she initially attributed to aging and her diabetes. Over time, these symptoms worsened, and she began to feel chest discomfort, described as pressure or heaviness in the center of her chest, particularly after meals or during emotional stress. Concerned, Mary consulted her primary care doctor, who ordered a stress test. During the test, she experienced chest pain, and her ECG revealed ischemic changes, indicating reduced blood flow to her heart. A coronary angiogram later showed significant blockages in two major coronary arteries, including one with an 85% blockage. Due to her diabetes and the severity of the blockages, Mary was not an ideal candidate for stenting alone. After consulting with her cardiologist, she underwent coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG), a surgery that reroutes blood around the blocked arteries using vessels from another part of her body. Post-surgery, Mary was placed on a strict diabetes management regimen, including insulin, statins to lower cholesterol, and aspirin to prevent blood clots. She was also referred to a nutritionist for dietary guidance and a physical therapist for a light exercise program tailored to her condition. With improved diabetes control and a healthier lifestyle, Mary's risk of another heart event decreased.
