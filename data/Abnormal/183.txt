Ahmed, a 48-year-old office worker, has a strong family history of heart disease, with both his father and older brother having suffered heart attacks in their early 50s. His father passed away from a cardiac event at 55, and his brother underwent a stent placement at 52 due to severe coronary artery disease. Despite being aware of this genetic risk, Ahmed had been a heavy smoker for over 25 years, consuming more than a pack of cigarettes daily, while leading a sedentary lifestyle and following a diet high in processed foods and saturated fats. Although he did not have diabetes or hypertension, he experienced gradual weight gain and was recently diagnosed with borderline high cholesterol. Over the past few months, he began experiencing mild chest discomfort, shortness of breath, dizziness, and fatigue, particularly after climbing stairs or prolonged walking, prompting him to visit a cardiologist. Diagnostic tests revealed elevated LDL cholesterol, reduced HDL cholesterol, minor ECG changes under stress, and moderate plaque buildup in his coronary arteries, suggesting early-stage coronary artery disease (CAD). His diagnosis was attributed to a combination of genetic predisposition and long-term smoking, which accelerated plaque formation in his arteries. His treatment plan focused on smoking cessation through a structured program, dietary improvements, increased physical activity, and medication, including statins, aspirin, and beta-blockers. After six months, Ahmed successfully reduced his smoking, improved his cholesterol levels, lost 4 kg, and reported fewer chest discomfort episodes with increased energy levels.
