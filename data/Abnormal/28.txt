Susan, a 72-year-old retired schoolteacher with a history of long-standing hypertension, mild asthma, and borderline high cholesterol, led an active life within her community but had limited physical activity due to her age and asthma. She did not experience any classic symptoms of heart disease, such as chest pain or shortness of breath. Instead, she reported mild fatigue and occasional dizziness, which she attributed to aging. During a routine check-up, her doctor noticed irregularities in her ECG and recommended further testing because of her risk factors. A stress test revealed poor blood flow to her heart during exertion, and a subsequent coronary angiogram confirmed that Susan had silent coronary artery disease, with an 80% blockage in her left circumflex artery. Despite the severity of the blockage, Susan had not experienced any noticeable chest pain, a condition known as "silent ischemia". Given her age and the extent of the blockage, Susan was treated with medication, including statins to lower her cholesterol, antihypertensives to control her blood pressure, and aspirin to reduce the risk of blood clots. She was not an immediate candidate for surgery or stenting but was closely monitored. Her treatment plan emphasized medical management and lifestyle adjustments, including light physical activity like walking and dietary changes to control her cholesterol. Regular follow-up appointments and tests were scheduled to monitor her condition. Although she had no overt symptoms, Susan's condition was caught early enough to prevent a heart attack through careful management.
