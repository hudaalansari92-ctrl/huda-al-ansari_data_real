John, a 50-year-old man with a history of poorly controlled hypertension, presented to the emergency room with sudden, intense chest pain that radiated to his left shoulder. His electrocardiogram (ECG) showed ST-segment elevation, suggesting an acute myocardial infarction (heart attack). Blood tests revealed significantly elevated troponin I levels, confirming cardiac muscle damage. An echocardiogram (echo) demonstrated reduced left ventricular function, with an ejection fraction of 35%, and hypokinesis (weak muscle movement) in the anterior wall of the heart. Coronary angiography revealed a 95% blockage in the left anterior descending artery (LAD). John underwent an emergency percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement to open the blocked artery. Post-procedure, his ECG normalized, and his troponin levels started to decrease. Follow-up echocardiography after a week showed improvement in heart function with an ejection fraction of 45%. He was prescribed beta-blockers, statins, aspirin, and Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors and was enrolled in a cardiac rehabilitation program.
