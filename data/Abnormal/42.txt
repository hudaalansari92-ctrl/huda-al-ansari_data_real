Mark, a 61-year-old male with a history of hypertension and borderline high cholesterol, presented to the emergency room after experiencing sudden, severe chest pain while mowing the lawn. The pain radiated to his jaw and left arm, accompanied by shortness of breath and nausea. Initial blood tests showed elevated troponin levels (TnI: 1.6 ng/mL), indicating heart muscle damage, while his cholesterol panel revealed elevated LDL (150 mg/dL), total cholesterol (250 mg/dL), and triglycerides (200 mg/dL), along with a fasting blood sugar of 110 mg/dL, suggesting borderline diabetes. His ECG displayed ST-segment elevation in the anterior leads (V2-V4), consistent with an acute ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI). An echocardiogram revealed a reduced left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) of 40%, with hypokinesis in the anterior wall of the heart. Mark was diagnosed with an acute anterior wall myocardial infarction due to severe coronary artery disease. An urgent coronary angiogram revealed a 90% blockage in the left anterior descending (LAD) artery, often referred to as the "widowmaker." A percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) was performed with stent placement, restoring blood flow. Post-procedure, Mark's ECG normalized, and his chest pain resolved. He was started on a regimen of aspirin, statins, beta-blockers, and Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors to manage his blood pressure and cholesterol. A follow-up echocardiogram after three months showed a slight improvement in his LVEF to 45%, and he was enrolled in a cardiac rehabilitation program. Lifestyle modifications, including a hearty diet and smoking cessation, were strongly emphasized to reduce future risks.
