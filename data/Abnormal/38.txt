David, a 45-year-old smoker with no prior medical history, collapsed at work after experiencing severe chest pain. Paramedics found him unresponsive with abnormal heart rhythms, and he was rushed to the emergency department. Upon arrival, his ECG showed ventricular fibrillation, and immediate defibrillation was required to restore a normal rhythm. Blood tests revealed extremely high troponin levels, indicating significant heart muscle damage. An emergent coronary angiogram showed a complete blockage of his right coronary artery (100%). He underwent emergency percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) to remove the blockage and place a stent. An echocardiogram performed the following day showed a severely reduced left ventricular ejection fraction of 30%, with akinesis (no movement) in the inferior wall of the heart, indicating extensive damage from the heart attack. Post-procedure, David was placed on a strict medication regimen, including beta-blockers, statins, aspirin, and antiplatelet therapy. He was also counseled on smoking cessation and lifestyle modifications and was referred to a cardiac rehabilitation program. A follow-up echo three months later showed minimal improvement in his heart function, and he remained at risk for future heart failure.
