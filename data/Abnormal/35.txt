Michael, a 62-year-old man with a history of hypertension and type 2 diabetes, came to the emergency department after experiencing persistent chest pain, tightness, and shortness of breath while climbing stairs. An initial electrocardiogram (ECG) revealed ST-segment depression, indicating possible ischemia (reduced blood flow to the heart). Blood tests showed elevated troponin levels, confirming myocardial injury, and an elevated LDL cholesterol level, a known risk factor for coronary artery disease. An echocardiogram (echo) was performed, revealing a reduced ejection fraction (EF) of 40%, suggesting left ventricular dysfunction, with hypokinesis (reduced movement) in the inferior wall of the heart, likely from impaired blood flow. Coronary angiography subsequently identified a 75% blockage in the right coronary artery and a 70% blockage in the left circumflex artery. Michael underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement to restore normal blood flow to these arteries. Post-procedure, his troponin levels gradually decreased, and his ECG showed resolution of the ischemic changes. A follow-up echocardiogram showed modest improvement in his ejection fraction. He was prescribed statins, Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, and aspirin and advised to make lifestyle changes to prevent future cardiovascular events.
