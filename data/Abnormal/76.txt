John, a 58-year-old man with a long history of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), primarily Crohn's disease, began experiencing chest pain during exercise. Over the years, his Crohn's disease had been managed with immunosuppressive therapy, but he often had flare-ups that required steroids. Given the chronic inflammation associated with IBD, John's doctor suspected coronary artery disease (CAD). Blood tests showed elevated troponin levels, and his ECG revealed signs of ischemia. An echocardiogram showed a reduced ejection fraction of 45%, and a coronary angiogram revealed a 75% blockage in the left circumflex artery. John was diagnosed with CAD and underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement. After the procedure, his chest pain improved, and he was started on statins, beta-blockers, and anti-inflammatory medications to better manage both his Crohn's disease and heart health.
