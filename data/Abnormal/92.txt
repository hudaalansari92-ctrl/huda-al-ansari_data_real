A 55-year-old man with a long-standing history of major depressive disorder and anxiety was admitted to the hospital with chest pain and shortness of breath. Although he had no traditional risk factors for coronary artery disease (CAD), he was a non-smoker, had a healthy weight, and his blood pressure and cholesterol levels were within normal ranges; his depression had contributed to sedentary behavior, poor dietary habits, and difficulty adhering to a healthy routine over the years. Despite attempts at various treatments for his mental health, his condition had led to increased inflammation and higher levels of stress hormones, factors known to affect cardiovascular health. Coronary angiography revealed significant CAD with blockages in two major arteries, necessitating stent placement. His cardiologist emphasized that the impact of chronic depression likely accelerated the atherosclerotic process. He was counselled on the importance of mental health treatment as part of his cardiac care. He was referred for therapy to manage his depression and support long-term cardiovascular health.
