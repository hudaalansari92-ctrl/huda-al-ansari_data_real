Michael, a 59-year-old man with a 15-year history of ankylosing spondylitis (AS), a chronic inflammatory disease affecting the spine, began experiencing chest pain and discomfort during mild physical activity. Over time, his AS had caused significant stiffness and inflammation in his joints, and he had been on long-term anti-inflammatory medication. His chest pain and shortness of breath prompted further evaluation. Blood tests revealed elevated troponin levels, suggesting heart muscle damage, and his ECG showed ST-segment depression. An echocardiogram revealed reduced heart function, with an ejection fraction of 48%, and a coronary angiogram confirmed a 70% blockage in his left anterior descending artery (LAD). Michael was diagnosed with coronary artery disease (CAD) likely related to chronic inflammation from his ankylosing spondylitis. He underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement and was started on statins, aspirin, and medications to better manage his inflammation and reduce the risk of future cardiovascular events.
