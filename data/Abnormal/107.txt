A 60-year-old man with a history of multiple sclerosis (MS), an autoimmune disease affecting the central nervous system, presented to his doctor with complaints of chest pain and fatigue, particularly during mild physical activities. Despite having no traditional risk factors for coronary artery disease (CAD), he was a non-smoker, maintained a healthy weight, and had normal cholesterol and blood pressure, and his MS had been active for over two decades, leading to recurrent inflammation and oxidative stress. Concerned, his physician performed an ECG and coronary angiography, which revealed significant CAD with a 65% blockage in one of his coronary arteries, necessitating a stent. His cardiologist explained that the chronic inflammation and immune system dysregulation associated with MS likely contributed to the development of CAD, accelerating atherosclerosis in the absence of other risk factors. Following treatment, he was advised to continue managing his MS effectively, adhere to a heart-healthy lifestyle, and attend regular follow-ups to monitor his cardiovascular health and prevent future complications.
