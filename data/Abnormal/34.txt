Robert, a 58-year-old man with a history of high cholesterol and smoking, presented to the emergency room with severe chest pain radiating to his left arm, shortness of breath, and sweating. Upon arrival, an electrocardiogram (ECG) was performed, showing ST-segment elevation, a clear sign of an acute myocardial infarction (heart attack). Blood tests revealed elevated troponin levels, confirming heart muscle damage. An echocardiogram (echo) was then performed, which showed reduced left ventricular function and areas of hypokinesia (weakened heart muscle movement) in the anterior wall, indicating significant damage to that part of the heart. Coronary angiography later revealed a critical 90% blockage in the left anterior descending artery (LAD), also known as the "widowmaker." Robert underwent emergency percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement to restore blood flow. Post-procedure, he was started on medications, including aspirin, statins, and beta-blockers. His troponin levels gradually declined, and follow-up echocardiography showed some improvement in heart function, though the damage remained significant.
