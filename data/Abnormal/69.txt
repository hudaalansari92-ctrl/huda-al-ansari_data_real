Emma, a 62-year-old woman, had been living with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) for over 20 years. Despite regular immunosuppressive therapy to manage her condition, she frequently experienced flare-ups, including joint pain, fatigue, and inflammation. Recently, she began to experience chest pain and shortness of breath, which worsened during physical activity. Given the known association between SLE and increased cardiovascular risk due to chronic inflammation, her doctor ordered a series of tests. Blood tests showed elevated troponin levels, indicating heart muscle damage, and her ECG revealed signs of ischemia. An echocardiogram confirmed reduced heart function, with an ejection fraction of 45%, and a coronary angiogram showed significant blockages, including an 80% blockage in the left anterior descending (LAD) artery. Emma was diagnosed with coronary artery disease (CAD) and underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement. Post-procedure, her symptoms improved, and she was started on statins, aspirin, and beta-blockers to manage her CAD alongside her lupus treatment.
