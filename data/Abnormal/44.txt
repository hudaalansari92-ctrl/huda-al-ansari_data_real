David, a 45-year-old man with no significant past medical history but a 20-year history of heavy smoking, arrived at the emergency room after experiencing sudden chest pain, shortness of breath, and sweating while at work. The chest pain, described as a crushing sensation, caused him to become increasingly anxious. Blood tests revealed markedly elevated troponin levels (TnI: 6.5 ng/mL), confirming substantial heart muscle damage, and his lipid profile showed elevated LDL cholesterol (190 mg/dL) and total cholesterol (280 mg/dL). An ECG showed ST-segment elevation in the inferior leads (II, III, aVF), consistent with an inferior wall myocardial infarction. An echocardiogram revealed an ejection fraction of 35%, with akinesis of the inferior wall, indicating significant heart muscle damage. David was diagnosed with an acute inferior wall STEMI caused by coronary artery disease. An urgent coronary angiogram revealed a 100% blockage of the right coronary artery (RCA), and a percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with successful stent placement was performed. Post-procedure, his ECG normalized, and his chest pain resolved. David was started on aspirin, beta-blockers, and statins and was strongly advised to quit smoking. A follow-up echocardiogram showed partial recovery of heart function, with an ejection fraction of 45%. He was enrolled in a smoking cessation program and cardiac rehabilitation to improve his long-term outcomes.
