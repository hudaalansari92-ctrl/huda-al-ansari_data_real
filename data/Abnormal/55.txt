Linda, a 70-year-old woman with stage 3 chronic kidney disease (CKD), had been living with hypertension and high cholesterol for many years. She had developed CKD as a result of long-term poorly controlled hypertension, which had caused progressive damage to her kidneys. Chronic kidney disease significantly increases the risk of coronary artery disease because of its association with hypertension, dyslipidemia, and inflammation. CKD patients often experience accelerated atherosclerosis, making them prone to heart disease. Linda reported feeling unusually fatigued and experiencing episodes of shortness of breath. She also had swelling in her legs, which she thought was due to her kidney condition. Blood tests revealed elevated troponin levels (0.7 ng/mL), indicating potential heart damage, and her lipid profile showed an LDL cholesterol of 160 mg/dL. Her ECG revealed left ventricular hypertrophy, and her echocardiogram showed an ejection fraction of 45% with mild hypokinesis in the lateral wall. Coronary angiography revealed a 70% blockage in her left circumflex artery. Linda underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement to open the blocked artery. Due to her CKD, she was carefully managed with medications, including Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors to control her blood pressure, statins to lower her cholesterol, and a low-sodium diet to protect her kidneys. Follow-up showed stabilization of her kidney function and improvement in her heart symptoms.
