John, a 54-year-old long-distance truck driver with a 20+ year smoking history and poorly controlled hypertension, had a family history of heart disease. His sedentary lifestyle and diet high in processed foods added to his risk factors. He began experiencing intermittent chest pain, particularly while driving, climbing stairs, or after meals. The pain often radiated to his left arm and was accompanied by shortness of breath and sweating, which he initially dismissed as heartburn or fatigue from long hours on the road. One night, after experiencing prolonged chest pain lasting more than 20 minutes, John was rushed to the emergency department by his family. An electrocardiogram (ECG) revealed changes consistent with a myocardial infarction (heart attack), and elevated troponin levels confirmed heart muscle damage. Diagnosed with acute coronary syndrome (ACS), John underwent an emergency coronary angiogram, which showed multiple blockages in his coronary arteries, including a 90% blockage in the left anterior descending (LAD) artery, often referred to as the "widowmaker" due to its association with fatal heart attacks. He received urgent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stents to open the blocked arteries. Following the procedure, John was prescribed medications, including beta-blockers, statins, aspirin, and Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, to manage his blood pressure and cholesterol and to prevent further clot formation. He was strongly advised to quit smoking and referred to a cardiac rehabilitation program. Despite initial difficulties, John made gradual improvements in his diet and successfully quit smoking, significantly reducing his risk of future cardiac events.
