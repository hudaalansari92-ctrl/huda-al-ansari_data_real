Frank, a 72-year-old man with stage 4 chronic kidney disease (CKD) resulting from long-standing hypertension, presented with increased fatigue and mild chest discomfort. His kidney function had been deteriorating for several years, which also raised his risk of cardiovascular complications. CKD is strongly associated with coronary artery disease due to the overlapping risk factors of hypertension and inflammation. After experiencing chest pain while doing light yard work, Frank sought medical attention. Blood tests revealed elevated troponin levels (TnI: 0.9 ng/mL) and worsening kidney function, with an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) of 25 mL/min. His ECG showed ischemic changes, and an echocardiogram revealed a reduced ejection fraction of 50% with signs of left ventricular hypertrophy. Coronary angiography identified a 70% blockage in his left circumflex artery. Due to his CKD, Frank was carefully managed with PCI and stent placement, and his medication regimen included statins and antihypertensives. Post-procedure, his kidney function stabilized, and his cardiovascular symptoms improved.
